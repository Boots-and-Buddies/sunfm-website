I had a client, software engineer at a big San Jose tech company, who was training with me three days a week and making zero progress. His workouts were solid. Sleep was decent. But every time we talked about food, same story: breakfast was coffee, lunch was whatever the office cafeteria had, dinner was DoorDash at 9 PM because he was too wiped to cook.

If that sounds like you, keep reading.

Most of the meal prep advice out there is built for people with free time. Color-coded containers, seventeen ingredients, four hours on Sunday. That doesn't work when you're pulling long weeks and still trying to train. What I'm going to share is the stripped-down version that my clients in San Jose and the South Bay actually stick with week after week.

## Why meal prep matters more when you train

A lot of people think meal prep is about "eating clean." It's really not. If you're training, meal prep is about getting protein and carbs into your body at the right times so your muscles can actually recover and your energy doesn't crater at 3 PM.

When you train hard and eat randomly:

- Recovery slows down because your muscles aren't getting protein when they need it
- Your energy dies mid-afternoon, which kills motivation to train after work
- You overeat at dinner because you've been running on fumes all day
- You lose muscle and hold onto fat, which is the opposite of why you're in the gym

I've watched this play out hundreds of times. The people who start prepping, even a basic version, see changes within two to three weeks that months of just training couldn't produce.

## The 5-container method

Forget prepping every meal for the whole week. That's how you burn out on Sunday and throw away sad tupperware on Thursday. Instead, prep five lunches and leave everything else flexible.

Why lunches? Because lunch is where busy people make their worst food decisions. You're mid-workday, stressed, and the easiest option is the burrito place down the street or just skipping it. Five prepped lunches takes that daily decision off your plate (literally) and keeps you fueled for afternoon training.

### How it works

Pick one protein, one carb, one vegetable. Don't overcomplicate this.

Combinations that work well:

- Chicken thighs + jasmine rice + roasted broccoli
- Ground turkey + sweet potatoes + sauteed spinach
- Salmon fillets + quinoa + roasted bell peppers
- Steak strips + brown rice + green beans
- Shrimp + rice noodles + snap peas and carrots

Cook everything on one sheet pan or in two pots. Sunday afternoon, 60 to 90 minutes, done. A few of my clients do this while watching football or half-listening to a podcast. Keeping it boring is the point.

For portions, each container should have roughly a palm-sized amount of protein (30-40g), a fist-sized amount of carbs, and two fists of vegetables. You don't need to weigh anything. If you're training regularly and eating this way, your body composition will move in the right direction.

## What to eat around your workouts

Timing matters here, not in the obsessive "anabolic window" way, but in the practical sense of how you feel during your session.

### Pre-workout (1-2 hours before)

You want carbs and moderate protein. Fat slows digestion, and that's the last thing you need before goblet squats.

Good options:
- Rice cake with banana and a thin layer of peanut butter
- Oatmeal with berries
- A small wrap with turkey and rice

If you train right after work and your last meal was lunch at noon, grab something small 30 to 45 minutes before. A banana, some dried mango, a small granola bar. Training on empty after an eight-hour gap is why so many people feel weak in their evening sessions.

### Post-workout (within 2 hours)

This is when protein matters most. Your muscles are ready to absorb amino acids and start repairing. Shoot for 30 to 40 grams of protein and some carbs.

If you train before dinner, just make sure dinner has protein. If you train in the morning, your prepped lunch covers it. If you train at lunch, keep a protein shake or Greek yogurt at the office.

## The 20-minute grocery run

One thing I've noticed: my South Bay clients either spend 90 minutes wandering Whole Foods or they don't go at all. Here's a list that covers your five prepped lunches plus breakfast and snack basics:

Proteins (pick 2): 2 lbs chicken thighs, 1 lb ground turkey, 1 lb salmon, 1 lb flank steak, or 1 lb shrimp.

Carbs (pick 2): jasmine or brown rice (one bag lasts weeks), sweet potatoes (4-5), quinoa, or rice noodles.

Vegetables (pick 3): broccoli (2 crowns), bell peppers (4-5), spinach (one bag), green beans (one bag), or zucchini (3-4).

Staples: eggs (one dozen), oats, bananas, Greek yogurt, olive oil, salt, pepper, garlic.

You can fill that cart in under 20 minutes at Safeway, Trader Joe's, or Ranch 99, all within a short drive if you're anywhere in San Jose.

## Mistakes I keep seeing

**Prepping food you don't like.** If you hate plain chicken breast, stop prepping plain chicken breast. Use thighs. Add sauce. Season it. The meal you actually eat is always better than the "optimal" meal you throw out Wednesday.

**Not prepping enough.** I can't count how many times someone tells me they prepped but ran out by Tuesday. If you're training hard, you need more food than you think. Leftovers beat the vending machine at 3 PM.

**Ignoring snacks.** Your lunches are the foundation, but you need something for the gaps. Keep mixed nuts, protein bars (20g+ protein, under 10g sugar), beef jerky, apples, or string cheese at your desk or in your bag.

**Turning it into a project.** The second meal prep starts feeling like a culinary event, you'll quit. Simple and repeatable is what works. Save the creative cooking for date night.

## What a real week looks like

One of my clients is a product manager in her mid-30s who trains with me Monday, Wednesday, and Friday mornings in San Jose. Here's her routine:

Sunday she spends about 75 minutes prepping. Bakes chicken thighs, roasts sweet potatoes and broccoli, portions everything into five containers, and hard-boils a dozen eggs.

Breakfast Monday through Friday is two hard-boiled eggs and oatmeal with banana. Five minutes.

Lunch is the prepped container. Two minutes in the microwave at the office.

On training days she makes a shake before work: protein powder, frozen berries, spinach, almond milk.

Dinner is flexible. She cooks something simple three or four nights and orders out once or twice. Because breakfast and lunch are already handled, dinner doesn't need to be perfect.

She dropped 12 pounds in three months without counting a single calorie. Not from any special formula. She just stopped making food decisions when she was tired and stressed.

## Start this week

You don't need to overhaul your diet. This Sunday, pick one protein, one carb, one vegetable. Cook them simply. Put them in containers. Do that for two weeks and see what happens. Most people notice they have more energy during workouts and they're not reaching for junk in the afternoon.
